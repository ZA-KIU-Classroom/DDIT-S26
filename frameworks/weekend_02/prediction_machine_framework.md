# The Prediction Machine Framework

## Source
Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb. Power and Prediction (2022).

## Core argument

AI is fundamentally a prediction technology. When you understand this, you can identify exactly which parts of any decision-making process are most exposed to AI.

## Four components of every decision

| Component | Definition | AI's role |
|---|---|---|
| Prediction | Estimating what will happen if a particular action is taken | AI dramatically reduces the cost of prediction |
| Judgment | Deciding which outcome is desirable given a prediction | Human domain. AI cannot determine what you want. |
| Action | Executing the chosen option | Increasingly automatable as prediction and judgment improve |
| Outcome | What actually happens after the action | The feedback loop that improves future predictions |

## Strategic implication

When AI makes prediction cheap, prediction separates from judgment. Before AI, humans bundled prediction and judgment in the same role. A loan officer both assessed credit risk (prediction) and decided whether to approve the loan (judgment). When prediction is cheap, the loan officer's value shifts entirely to judgment.

## Power shift

If prediction determines competitive advantage, the strategic question becomes: who has the data to make the best predictions? Data ownership and quality become the primary source of competitive advantage.

## How to use in this course

1. Identify which predictions in the client's value chain are getting cheaper.
2. Identify whose judgment becomes more valuable when those predictions are cheap.
3. Assess who controls the data that produces the best predictions.
4. Connect this to the AI Opportunity Map and strategic options evaluation.
